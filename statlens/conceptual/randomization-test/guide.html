Instructor Guide: What is a Randomization Test?

Companion notes for the StatLens conceptual activity — IMS Chapters 11 & 17

Launch activity in Presentation Mode

Overview ~20 min

This 5-step activity builds intuition for the logic of a randomization test using a card-shuffle metaphor. Students see the original data as colored cards, then watch what happens when group labels are randomly reassigned. The activity works with three IMS datasets (sex discrimination, opportunity cost, CPR/blood thinner) and can be switched live during class.

The activity is aligned with the IMS "Introduction to Modern Statistics" approach: observe a difference → simulate the null → compare → conclude.

Presentation mode shows all steps at once with no quiz gates. Use ← / → arrow keys to scroll between steps. Students can follow along on their own devices in Discovery mode (default link).

Step 1: The Observed Data ~4 min

Ask the class: "What do you notice about the difference in promotion rates between the two groups? Is it large enough to matter?"

Key points to cover:

  • The cards represent individual observations. Red = success outcome (promoted, survived, etc.); gray = failure outcome.
  • Draw attention to the two-way table. The success row is highlighted in pink, failure in gray — matching the card colors.
  • State the observed difference explicitly (e.g., 87.5% − 58.3% = 29.2 pp for sex discrimination).
  • Frame the core question: "Is this difference real, or could it be a coincidence?"
Common misconception: Students often think any visible difference must be meaningful. Point out that even with no real effect, random assignment alone produces some difference.

Step 2: Could This Be Due to Chance? ~3 min

Ask: "If sex truly had no effect, and we re-ran the experiment, would we expect the exact same difference? Why not?"

Key points:

  • State H₀ and Hₐ in plain language. The null hypothesis says the group labels don't matter.
  • Emphasize the "Key idea" box on screen: if the explanatory variable has no effect, the outcomes are fixed. The only random part is which group each person is in.
  • This is the conceptual leap: shuffling simulates what the world looks like under H₀.
Common misconception: "The null hypothesis says the groups are exactly equal." Clarify: H₀ says there is no systematic effect — random variation still produces some difference.

Step 3: Shuffle and Re-deal ~4 min

Ask before shuffling: "If I shuffle these cards and re-deal them into two groups, what difference do you predict? Write it down."

Key points:

  • Have students lock in a prediction before clicking "Shuffle once." This creates investment in the result.
  • After the shuffle: "How does this simulated difference compare to the observed? Is the observed difference unusual?"
  • Click shuffle 2–3 times. Ask if students see a pattern.
  • One shuffle is suggestive but not conclusive — this motivates Step 4.
Tip: If using the sex discrimination dataset, the observed difference (29.2 pp) is quite large. Most shuffles will produce differences near 0. This makes the visual contrast striking.

Step 4: Build the Null Distribution ~5 min

Ask: "What shape do you expect the distribution to be? Where should it be centered? Why?"

Sequence of actions:

  1. Click +1 a few times — watch dots appear one at a time.
  2. Click +10 — histogram starts to take shape.
  3. Click +100 — bell shape becomes clear, centered near 0.
  4. Click +1000 — smooth distribution, red line (observed) clearly in the tail.

Key points:

  • The red vertical line marks the observed difference. Ask where it falls relative to the distribution.
  • The p-value is the proportion of simulations as extreme as the observed. It appears in the stats line.
  • "The histogram shows us what the world looks like if H₀ is true. The red line shows what actually happened."
After ~1000 shuffles: "What proportion of the simulated differences were as large as the observed? Is the observed difference typical or unusual under the null hypothesis?"
Common misconception: "The p-value is the probability that H₀ is true." Correct framing: "The p-value is the probability of observing a result this extreme if H₀ were true."

Step 5: Draw a Conclusion ~4 min

Ask: "Based on the p-value and the histogram, what do you conclude? Would your conclusion change with a different significance level? What if the sample were larger?"

Key points:

  • The model conclusion uses strength-of-evidence language (very strong, strong, moderate, little to no evidence).
  • Emphasize the interpretation box — it translates the statistical result into the context of the research question.
  • Connect back to the original question: "So, do we have evidence of discrimination / an effect / a treatment benefit?"
Extension: Switch to a different dataset (e.g., CPR) and re-run. Ask students to predict whether the conclusion will be the same. The CPR dataset gives a non-significant result — great for showing that not every study produces strong evidence.

Dataset Notes

Sex Discrimination (default)

  • Result: Strong evidence of discrimination (p ≈ 0.02). Observed difference ~29.2 pp.
  • Why this dataset: Classic IMS example, dramatic visual, clear result.

Opportunity Cost

  • Result: Moderate-to-strong evidence (p ≈ 0.02–0.04). The reminder matters.
  • Why this dataset: Relatable to students (buying decisions), treatment effect is moderate.

CPR & Blood Thinner

  • Result: Weak/no evidence (p ≈ 0.25+). The blood thinner doesn't clearly help.
  • Why this dataset: Important to show a non-significant result. Prevents students from thinking every study "proves" something.

Learning Objectives

  1. Students can explain why we shuffle: to simulate the null hypothesis.
  2. Students can interpret a null distribution: "what the world looks like if H₀ is true."
  3. Students can locate the observed statistic on the null distribution and assess whether it's unusual.
  4. Students can interpret a p-value as the proportion of simulations as extreme as the observed.
  5. Students can state a conclusion in context using strength-of-evidence language.

Common Questions from Students

"Why do we shuffle instead of just calculating a p-value?"

The simulation makes the abstract concept visible. Later, we'll learn formulas that approximate what the simulation does, but the simulation builds the intuition first.

"How many shuffles do we need?"

More is better, but 1,000 is usually enough for a good estimate. The p-value stabilizes. Try running 100 vs. 1,000 and note how the p-value barely changes.

"What if I get a different p-value each time?"

Each set of simulations is random, so exact p-values will vary slightly. The conclusion (reject or fail to reject) should be consistent for clear-cut cases.
